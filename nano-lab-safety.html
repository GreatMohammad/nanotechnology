About This Training

This comprehensive safety module is designed to prepare you for hands-on work in nanotechnology laboratories. While the labs in this manual are designed to be low-cost and accessible, safety remains our top priority.

Why Safety Matters in Nanotechnology

Nanotechnology labs involve unique hazards including:

  • Chemical exposure - Working with etchants, solvents, and reactive materials
  • High-temperature equipment - Hotplates, thermal evaporation systems, and plasma generation
  • Vacuum systems - Potential implosion hazards and pressure differentials
  • Electrical hazards - Power supplies, UV exposure systems, and electrodeposition
  • Mechanical hazards - Rotating equipment, high-speed fans, and cutting tools

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Safety Principle

Nearly all labs have some level of risk involved in their completion, however this risk is minimal when proper lab safety protocols are followed.

Your safety depends on understanding hazards, following procedures, and maintaining awareness at all times.

Chemical Safety

Common Laboratory Chemicals

The following chemicals are used throughout the lab manual. Understanding their hazards is essential:

Acetone

Used in: Labs 6, 7, 8 (cleaning/developing)

Hazards: Flammable, volatile, irritant

Safety:

  • Use in well-ventilated area
  • No open flames nearby
  • Avoid skin contact

Isopropyl Alcohol (IPA)

Used in: Labs 6, 7, 8, 10

Hazards: Flammable, eye/skin irritant

Safety:

  • Keep away from heat sources
  • Use in ventilated area
  • Rinse immediately if contact occurs

Ferric Chloride (Iron III Chloride)

Used in: Labs 7, 8 (wet etching)

Hazards: Corrosive, causes skin burns, stains

Safety:

  • Wear chemical-resistant gloves
  • Use under fume hood if possible
  • Dilute as directed by instructor

Hydrogen Tetrachloroaurate (HAuCl‚ÇÑ)

Used in: Labs 9, 10 (gold deposition)

Hazards: Corrosive, severe eye damage

Safety:

  • Chemical splash goggles required
  • Chemical-resistant gloves mandatory
  • Instructor should prepare solution

Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)

Used in: Lab 10

Hazards: Strong base, corrosive

Safety:

  • Causes severe burns
  • Use extreme caution
  • Rinse immediately if contact occurs

üö® Critical Chemical Safety Rules

  1. Read Safety Data Sheets (SDS) before handling any chemical
  2. Never pipette by mouth - always use pipette bulbs or pumps
  3. Add acid to water, never water to acid
  4. Label all containers immediately after transferring chemicals
  5. Dispose properly - never pour chemicals down the drain unless approved
  6. Work in ventilated areas - use fume hoods when available
  7. Report all spills immediately, no matter how small

Equipment-Specific Safety

Vacuum Systems (Labs 2, 3, 4, 5)

Implosion Hazard

Vacuum systems create significant pressure differentials. Glassware under vacuum can implode if:

  • Glass has cracks or defects
  • Temperature changes too rapidly
  • Pressure exceeds rated limits

Prevention: Always inspect glassware before use. Wear safety glasses. Never exceed temperature limits.

Safe Operating Procedures

  • System must be operated in dry environment
  • Keep fingers clear when tightening screws/clamps
  • Two people should lift heavy systems
  • Never lean against or push the system
  • Allow system to cool before handling after plasma/heating operations

High-Temperature Equipment (Labs 4, 5, 6, 7, 8)

üî• Burn Prevention

Multiple labs involve hotplates and heating elements reaching 155¬∞C (311¬∞F) or higher:

  • Lab 4 (Plasma): Flask can exceed 750¬∞F - wait minimum 5 minutes to cool
  • Lab 5 (Thermal Evaporation): Heating elements and substrates become very hot
  • Labs 6, 7, 8: Hotplates at 155¬∞C for baking steps

Safety Measures:

  • Always use heat-resistant gloves or forceps to handle hot items
  • Never touch glass or metal that has been heated without testing temperature first
  • Allow adequate cooling time as specified in procedures
  • Keep flammable materials away from hot surfaces
  • Know the location of burn treatment supplies

Plasma Systems (Lab 4)

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Plasma Safety

Lab 4 creates plasma using a microwave and vacuum system. This involves multiple hazards:

Implosion Risk:

  • Pressure between 80-120 mTorr can create plasma exceeding 750¬∞F
  • This can cause flask meltdown and implosion
  • Keep pressure below 50 mTorr or above 120 mTorr
  • Never exceed 30 seconds total application time

Inhalation Hazard:

  • Plasma generates free radicals and reactive species
  • Must be performed under fume hood
  • Wait for cooling before opening chamber

Eye Protection:

  • Wear safety glasses when observing plasma
  • Never stare directly at bright plasma

UV Exposure Systems (Labs 6, 8)

UV Radiation Hazard

UV nail curing systems are used for photolithography exposure. UV radiation can damage eyes and skin.

Protection Required:

  • Never look directly at UV light during exposure
  • UV-protecting eyewear required when observing
  • Minimize skin exposure to UV
  • Follow exposure time limits strictly

Spin Coating (Labs 6, 8)

Rotating Equipment Safety

  • Keep hands clear of spinning fan/coater
  • Secure substrate properly before spinning
  • Wear safety glasses - droplets may fly off
  • Perform in well-ventilated area (solvents evaporate)
  • Allow fan to stop completely before approaching

Electrochemical Systems (Labs 9, 10)

Electrical and Chemical Hazards

Electrodeposition combines electrical current with corrosive chemicals:

Electrical Safety:

  • Inspect wires and connections before use
  • Keep electrical components away from water/spills
  • Never touch electrodes while power is on
  • Disconnect power before handling electrodes

Chemical Safety:

  • HAuCl‚ÇÑ is highly corrosive - causes severe eye damage
  • Chemical splash goggles mandatory
  • Chemical-resistant gloves required
  • Work under fume hood

General Laboratory Safety

Pre-Lab Preparation

Working in the Laboratory

Best Practices

  • Never work alone - Always have someone nearby who can help in emergencies
  • Stay focused - No eating, drinking, or applying cosmetics in lab
  • Secure long hair - Tie back to prevent contact with chemicals or equipment
  • Remove jewelry - Rings, bracelets can catch on equipment or conduct electricity
  • Keep aisles clear - Ensure emergency exits are accessible
  • Clean as you go - Maintain organized, uncluttered workspace
  • Label everything - All containers, samples, and materials

Cleanroom Protocols (Lab 1)

Particulate Control

Lab 1 introduces cleanroom concepts. While building a miniature cleanroom is safe, understanding contamination control is important:

  • Sawdust/particulates - wear safety goggles to avoid eye irritation
  • Computer fans - keep fingers away from spinning blades
  • Cutting cardboard - use appropriate tools, cut away from body
  • 9V battery connections - avoid short circuits

Glassware and Sharp Object Safety

Cut Prevention

Multiple labs involve cutting glass slides and working with sharp tools:

Glass Cutting (Labs 4, 6, 7, 8):

  • Use proper glass cutters and diamond scribes
  • Score once - do not re-score the same line
  • Snap away from body
  • Edges will be sharp - handle carefully
  • Dispose of broken glass in designated sharps container

If Cut Occurs:

  • Apply pressure to stop bleeding
  • Rinse with clean water
  • Notify instructor immediately
  • Seek medical attention if cut is deep or won't stop bleeding

Fume Hood Usage

When Fume Hoods Are Required

Fume hoods provide ventilation and containment for chemical vapors:

Mandatory for:

  • Lab 4 - Plasma generation (free radicals)
  • Lab 6, 8 - Spin coating with acetone
  • Lab 7, 8 - Etching with ferric chloride
  • Lab 9, 10 - Working with HAuCl‚ÇÑ

Proper Use:

  • Keep sash at recommended height
  • Work at least 6 inches inside hood
  • Don't block airflow with equipment
  • Leave hood running during and after work
  • Never put head inside while chemicals are present

If fume hood unavailable: Respirator required and work in well-ventilated area with windows open

Emergency Procedures

üö® Emergency Contact Information

Before starting any lab, know the location of:

  • Emergency phone and campus security number
  • Fire alarm pull stations
  • Emergency exits
  • Fire extinguishers
  • Eyewash stations
  • Safety showers
  • First aid kit
  • Spill response kit

Emergency Response by Type

üî• Fire Emergency

Small fire (contained):

  1. Alert others immediately
  2. If trained, use appropriate fire extinguisher
  3. Evacuate if fire spreads

Large fire or spreading:

  1. Activate fire alarm
  2. Evacuate immediately
  3. Close doors behind you
  4. Call emergency services (911)
  5. Do NOT re-enter building

Clothing on fire:

  • STOP, DROP, and ROLL
  • Use safety shower if available
  • Smother flames with fire blanket

üëÅÔ∏è Eye Exposure

Critical - Minutes matter!

  1. Immediately go to eyewash station
  2. Hold eyelids open and flush for 15 minutes minimum
  3. Remove contact lenses if possible
  4. Have someone notify instructor/emergency services
  5. Seek medical attention immediately after flushing

Do NOT:

  • Rub eyes
  • Use neutralizing solutions
  • Delay flushing to find instructor

üß¥ Chemical Skin Contact

Immediate action required:

  1. Remove contaminated clothing
  2. Flush affected area with water for 15 minutes
  3. Use safety shower for large areas
  4. Notify instructor
  5. Seek medical attention for:
    • Strong acids/bases (HCl, NaOH, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ)
    • Large area exposure
    • Blistering or severe pain

Special cases:

  • HAuCl‚ÇÑ - Highly corrosive, flush immediately and seek medical attention
  • Ferric chloride - Will stain skin, flush thoroughly

üî• Burn Treatment

Thermal burns (hot plates, plasma systems):

  1. Remove from heat source immediately
  2. Cool burn with cool (not ice cold) running water for 10-15 minutes
  3. Remove jewelry/tight clothing near burn
  4. Cover with sterile, non-stick bandage
  5. Do NOT apply ice, butter, or ointments
  6. Seek medical attention for:
    • Burns larger than 3 inches
    • Burns on face, hands, feet, genitals
    • Third-degree burns (white/charred)
    • Blistering burns

‚ö° Electrical Shock

If someone is being shocked:

  1. DO NOT TOUCH the person
  2. Turn off power source if safe
  3. If unable to turn off, use non-conductive object to separate person from source
  4. Call 911 immediately
  5. Check breathing and pulse
  6. Begin CPR if trained and needed

Prevention:

  • Inspect cords before use
  • Keep electrical equipment away from water
  • Never use equipment with frayed cords
  • Unplug by pulling plug, not cord

üí® Inhalation Exposure

If chemical vapors are inhaled:

  1. Move to fresh air immediately
  2. Alert others in area
  3. Loosen tight clothing
  4. Sit upright if breathing is difficult
  5. Notify instructor
  6. Call 911 if:
    • Difficulty breathing persists
    • Dizziness or confusion
    • Loss of consciousness

Do NOT:

  • Re-enter contaminated area without protection
  • Induce vomiting

üí• Glass Implosion/Breakage

If vacuum flask implodes (Lab 4 risk):

  1. Stand back immediately
  2. Turn off all heating/power
  3. Alert instructor
  4. Check for injuries from flying glass
  5. Do NOT clean up - wait for instructor
  6. Ventilate area if chemicals released

Prevention:

  • Inspect glassware for cracks before use
  • Follow temperature/pressure limits strictly
  • Allow proper cooling time
  • Never exceed 30 seconds microwave time (Lab 4)
  • Keep pressure below 50 mTorr or above 120 mTorr for plasma

‚ò£Ô∏è Chemical Spill Response

Minor spill (less than 100mL):

  1. Alert people in immediate area
  2. Wear appropriate PPE
  3. Contain spill with absorbent materials
  4. Clean up using spill kit
  5. Dispose in appropriate waste container
  6. Notify instructor

Major spill (>100mL or highly hazardous):

  1. Alert everyone in area
  2. Evacuate and close doors
  3. Notify instructor and campus safety
  4. Do NOT attempt cleanup
  5. Wait for trained personnel

If spill contacts person, treat as skin/eye exposure emergency (see above)

Evacuation Procedures

When to Evacuate

  • Fire alarm activation
  • Gas leak or strong chemical odor
  • Explosion or structural damage
  • When instructed by emergency personnel

Evacuation Steps

  1. Stop work immediately and safely shut down equipment if time permits
  2. Leave belongings behind
  3. Walk, do not run, to nearest exit
  4. Use stairs, never elevators
  5. Help others if possible
  6. Close doors behind you
  7. Proceed to designated assembly area
  8. Report missing persons to emergency personnel
  9. Do NOT re-enter until authorized

Key Takeaways
